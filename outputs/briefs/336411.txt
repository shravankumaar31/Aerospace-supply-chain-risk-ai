# Risk Brief: NAICS 336411 — Aircraft Manufacturing

## Executive Summary

Aircraft Manufacturing presents a **Medium** composite risk (46.43/100), driven primarily by elevated export concentration to a single destination country. With 240,989 workers supporting over $10 billion in annual exports, this segment warrants active monitoring despite moderate overall workforce stability.

## Risk Drivers

- **Export destination dependency**: The 60.42/100 concentration risk score indicates that approximately 60% of the segment's $10.0 billion in exports flow to a single destination country, creating significant trade policy and geopolitical exposure.

- **Workforce stability buffer**: The 32.44/100 workforce risk score suggests moderate resilience in labor availability and retention, mitigating near-term production continuity concerns across the 240,989-person employment base.

- **Composite risk positioning**: At 46.43/100, the segment falls at the upper end of Medium tier, positioning it within 4 points of High-tier classification and indicating vulnerability to single-shock escalation.

## Market Context

Aircraft Manufacturing forms the apex of U.S. aerospace supply chains, delivering finished platforms to domestic military and commercial customers as well as allied nations. The segment's $10 billion export volume reflects the strategic importance of foreign military sales and international airline deliveries. However, the 60% concentration to one destination country — likely a major NATO ally or Asia-Pacific partner — introduces trade corridor fragility uncommon in other aerospace tiers. While the workforce risk remains moderate, the segment's scale (241,000 employees) means even small disruptions cascade rapidly through lower-tier suppliers. Regional clustering around historic airframe manufacturing hubs compounds exposure.

## Recommended Actions

- **Procurement teams** should conduct trade flow audits on sole-source aircraft contracts to quantify exposure to the dominant export destination and develop alternative delivery pathways or contract structures resilient to trade disruptions.

- **Program managers** must map subassembly sourcing for platforms destined to the primary export market, identifying critical path components vulnerable to re-export restrictions or tariff escalation.

- **Supply chain directors** should establish quarterly export diversity tracking, triggering contingency reviews if concentration exceeds 65% or if the primary destination signals policy changes affecting defense articles or dual-use technologies.
